Title: How to speak confidently in interviews
Category: interview_skills
Duration: ~8 minutes

---

Namaste, and welcome back. Today we're going to talk about something that makes a huge difference in interviews — but most people never practice it. We're not talking about your resume or your qualifications. We're talking about how you show up — your body, your voice, and your mindset — from the moment you walk in.
Let's start with body language, because this is where most people lose points before they say a single word.
When you walk into an interview room, the interviewer is already forming an impression. Research shows that people make judgments in the first 30 seconds. So what you do with your body in those first 30 seconds matters enormously.
First — posture. Sit upright, not stiff, but not slouching either. Think of it as sitting tall. Your spine should be straight, your shoulders relaxed. When you slouch, it signals discomfort and low confidence. When you sit upright, it signals readiness and respect.
Second — eye contact. This is one of the most powerful signals you can send. When someone asks you a question, look at them while they're speaking. When you answer, maintain eye contact naturally — not staring intensely, but not looking at the floor either. Looking away repeatedly signals nervousness or dishonesty, even if you're completely honest.
Third — hands. Keep your hands on the table or in your lap. Do not fold your arms — this creates a physical barrier. Do not fidget with your pen or phone. Still hands signal calm. Moving hands signal anxiety.
Now let's talk about your voice.
Voice modulation is about more than just speaking clearly. It's about pace, tone, and energy. Many people in interviews speak too fast when they're nervous — the words come out in a rush and the interviewer can't follow. Slow down. A slightly slower pace signals confidence.
Your tone matters too. A flat, monotone voice makes even brilliant answers sound boring. Vary your pitch — rise slightly when you make an important point, pause for emphasis. Practice reading something aloud and notice where you naturally pause and where you speed up.
Now, what about answering questions well?
The biggest mistake people make is answering without structure. They start talking, go in circles, and end up saying a lot without really saying anything. Interviewers notice this immediately.
The simplest structure you can use is: situation, then what you did, then the result. This is sometimes called the STAR method — Situation, Task, Action, Result. But don't get lost in the name. The principle is simple: tell me what was happening, tell me what YOU specifically did, and tell me what happened because of it.
For example, if someone asks "Tell me about a time you handled pressure" — don't just say "I am good under pressure." Say: "At my last job, we had a deadline move up by two days unexpectedly. I made a list of what absolutely had to be done, communicated with the team about what we could delay, and we delivered on time. My manager specifically mentioned this in my review." That is a structured answer. That is memorable.
Now — the thing no one talks about: nervousness.
Everyone is nervous in interviews. Everyone. Including the interviewer, who also wants the conversation to go well. The goal is not to eliminate nervousness — that is not possible and not necessary. The goal is to not let nervousness control your body or your words.
Here is what actually helps: breathe slowly before you answer. When a question comes, take one second — just one — before you start speaking. That pause signals that you are thinking, not panicking. It also gives your brain a moment to organize.
Another thing that helps: remind yourself before you go in that nervousness and excitement feel almost identical in the body. Your heart rate is up, your attention is sharp. That is actually your body preparing you to do well. Reframe it. You are not scared — you are ready.
So let's recap. Walk in with your shoulders back and your chin up. Sit upright. Make eye contact. Speak at a pace the interviewer can follow. Structure your answers — what happened, what you did, what the outcome was. And when you feel nervous, breathe, pause, and begin.
You have prepared. You know your work. Now let your body and your voice show it.
